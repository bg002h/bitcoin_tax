The statutory test survives direct attack — the citations are accurate, the extraction is careful, and the ASSERT/FORGO line correctly classifies eleven of the thirteen census fields. What blocks green is the one field where the test was applied to the wrong question (`hsa_present`, whose premise is false and whose specced behavior refuses valid returns), a type change that re-runs the exact D-8 laundering with no migration, a class-(B) design that cannot distinguish "answered No" from "never asked" and therefore cannot implement its own advisory contract, a census that stops at the input model while the form surface has at least one more silent single-box declaration (Schedule A line 8), a build order whose first three steps cannot compile, and a dead-field deletion whose stated mechanism ("breaking change") is factually backwards.

## The legal spine — attacked directly, and it holds (with two recorded caveats)

I verified the quotes against the statutes. §6065: *"shall contain or be verified by a written declaration that it is made under the penalties of perjury"* — accurate. §7206(1): *"which he does not believe to be true and correct as to every material matter"* — accurate, and the spec quotes the willfulness element rather than hiding it. *New Colonial Ice*, 292 U.S. at 440 — "legislative grace" and the burden-to-claim proposition are genuinely there and genuinely stand for what the spec uses them for; §63(f)'s additional amounts and the §164(b)(5) election are both deduction-side, so the extension is by fair analogy, not stretch.

Two caveats, recorded so the doctrine is not oversold, neither blocking:

1. **§7206(1) does not expose the unwitting filer.** Willfulness (*Cheek*) shields someone who never saw the box. The spec's sound half is the one it actually leans on — *"software cannot supply belief on their behalf"* — a design norm grounded in the §6065 jurat, not a prosecution theory. The spec should not be read (and does not quite claim) that the filer commits a felony; the real-world stakes are civil (§6662) plus the software's integrity claim. Fine as written.
2. **The ASSERT/FORGO dichotomy is clean on the census as classified.** I hunted for a BOTH field. `salt_use_sales_tax`: *checking* the box asserts an election; leaving it *unchecked* asserts nothing — "no election was made" is true of every non-elector. Clean (B). `blind`: the unchecked §63(f) box is claim-shaped — a blind filer who leaves it unchecked under-claims; nothing false is stated, and the deduction printed on line 12 is arithmetically consistent with the unchecked box. Clean (B). The dependent, dual-status, and MFS boxes all pair a factual assertion with an unlawfully-granted benefit — clean (A). `presidential_fund`: opt-in, immaterial to tax — clean (C). The dichotomy fails only where a *field* conflates two populations, which is the Critical below.

## Critical

### C-1 — `hsa_present` as specced refuses valid returns on a false premise. §2.4 must be re-scoped to the Form 8889 triggers, not HSA existence

§2.4's premise is *"an HSA needs Form 8889."* **That is false.** Form 8889 is required only when one of the filing triggers applies: contributions made for the year (by anyone, including an employer), distributions received, testing-period income inclusion, or acquisition by death of the beneficiary. A filer with a **dormant HSA** — funds sitting from a prior year, no contributions, no distributions — has no 8889 obligation from any authority: not the statute (§223 imposes no attachment absent a reportable item; §6011(a) reaches only what the forms require), not even the instructions. Their return omits nothing; the jurat's "complete" is intact.

Now trace the specced behavior. `HsaPresent` is live **always**; unanswered ⇒ refuse; and per §4's acceptance, *"`Some(true)` on either refuses as unsupported."* So every filer is asked "do you have an HSA?", and a truthful **"yes" from a dormant-HSA holder permanently refuses a return btctax computes correctly today** (currently that filer sails through, rightly, because nobody asks and nothing about their return touches an HSA). The only exits are answering falsely or leaving the app. That is a bricked valid return with a perjury-flavored escape hatch — the spec makes this population strictly worse while claiming to close a hole.

The real hole §2.4 gestures at is genuine but belongs to the **activity** population: a direct personal contribution (no box-12 W trace — the code-W backstop at `return_refuse.rs:28` is real and correctly recorded as partial) or a distribution (1099-SA, unmodeled) does omit a required form, and a distribution is an understatement channel. For *that* population, class (A) via the completeness prong is exactly right.

**Fix (small, spec-level):** scope the question to the 8889 triggers — *"In {year}, did you (or your employer) contribute to, or did you take a distribution from, a health savings account?"* — rename or re-document the field accordingly, keep unanswered ⇒ refuse and yes ⇒ refuse-unsupported. Then the class-(A) rationale is true of everyone who answers "yes," and no valid return is refused. §2.4's census row, §3.1's entry, and the two §4 acceptance bullets all change with it.

## Important

### I-1 — `hsa_present: bool → Option<bool>` re-runs the D-8 laundering: every stored row's `false` is ratified as an answered "No", and the spec schedules no migration

`Schedule1Inputs.hsa_present` serializes with no `skip_serializing_if`, so **every existing stored blob carries `"hsa_present": false`** (`crates/btctax-core/src/tax/return_inputs.rs:319-321`). Change the type to `Option<bool>` and serde reads that stored `false` as `Some(false)` — a never-asked default ratified as the filer's answer, which is verbatim the version-0 defect D-8 required `SCHEMA_VERSION` 1 plus the `row_to_inputs` unlaundering to cure (`crates/btctax-cli/src/return_inputs.rs:50-66`). The new refusal then never fires for exactly the population that has the bug — P8a I2's phrasing, and this time the direction is the bad one (an HSA distribution untaxed on a pre-P9 row). The spec demonstrably thought about migration — §2.5 argues `dual_status_alien` needs none because it is a *new* field ("`None` on an existing row is honest") — and then skipped the one field whose *type changes*. "There are no users" is not the discipline this codebase runs on: v0.2.0 is published, and the D-8 precedent is that stored-blob compatibility is handled by schema versions, not waved. **Fix:** `SCHEMA_VERSION` 2; `version < 2 ⇒ unlaunder(sch1.hsa_present)` in `row_to_inputs`, with the named v1-blob test the P8a I2 pattern demands. (A TOML that *explicitly writes* `hsa_present = false` is a typed answer and correctly loads `Some(false)` — only the stored-row path launders.)

### I-2 — Class (B) is unimplementable as specced: bare `bool`s cannot carry the asked/unasked distinction the advisory contract requires, and neither advisory has a firing predicate

The spec's own model case disproves its design. `AgedBoxForfeitedNoDob` fires on `date_of_birth.is_none()` (`crates/btctax-core/src/tax/advisories.rs:214-227`) — on **unknown**, never on known-young. It can do that because the field is an `Option`. The spec keeps `blind` and `salt_use_sales_tax` as bare `bool`s, then writes (§3.2): *"empty input leaves them `false`, which is lawful; the advisory then fires."* But an explicit "n" **also** leaves `false` — the same value. So `BlindBoxForfeitedNotDeclared` either fires on every sighted filer's every report **forever, including after they answered the new prompt**, or it never fires. Neither implements §2.1's contract, and the first turns the owner's mandate into permanent noise ("you may be forfeiting $1,550 by not declaring blindness" — to someone who just said they aren't blind). `SalesTaxElectionNotMade` is worse: the spec never states *when* it fires at all — on every return without the election? only when `schedule_a.is_some()`? only when the return actually itemized? only in no-income-tax states (we hold `address_state`)? §4 gates acceptance on advisories whose trigger is undefined. **Fix:** give class (B) the tri-state too — `blind: Option<bool>`, `salt_use_sales_tax: Option<bool>` (`None` = never asked ⇒ advisory; `Some(false)` = declined ⇒ silent; `Some(true)` = claimed) — which is exactly the DOB shape the spec calls "the model for it"; spec each advisory's predicate; and give both fields the same I-1 stored-row treatment (`false` from an old row ⇒ `None` — here the stake is only advisory noise, but the discipline is one rule). If the author instead *wants* fire-on-false, the spec must say so and defend the noise; it currently specifies neither.

### I-3 — The `salt_use_sales_tax` prompt is a footgun: "y" with no amount silently zeroes line 5a, and no guard exists in that direction

`salt_line_5a` (`crates/btctax-core/src/tax/return_1040.rs:110-116`): election on ⇒ 5a = `salt_sales_tax_amount` **only** — W-2 box 17/19 withholding and estimated payments drop out. `income answer` is a bool/date command; the specced class-(B) prompt sets the election with no way to capture the amount. A filer who answers "y" (perhaps thinking "yes, I'd like that deduction") with `salt_sales_tax_amount == 0` gets 5a = $0 and a silently collapsed SALT deduction. The existing guard is one-directional only — `SaltSalesTaxWithoutElection` catches amount-without-election (`return_refuse.rs:527-535`), nothing catches election-without-amount. Overstatement direction, but it is a *new hazardous write path this spec creates*, it violates the owner mandate's own spirit (a benefit — the income-tax SALT amounts — forgone in silence), and the spec is also silent on where `answer` even writes the bool when `schedule_a` is `None`. **Fix:** spec the symmetric guard (election on ∧ amount == 0 ∧ income-tax-path fields > 0 ⇒ refuse or advise), define the `schedule_a: None` behavior, or scope the prompt to returns that already carry a Schedule A.

### I-4 — The census stops at the input model, and the printed-form surface has at least one more silent single-box declaration: the Schedule A line 8 mixed-use-mortgage checkbox. The spec also never states the criterion that makes its own §2.5 principled

I swept every checkbox on the seven supported forms. One fails the spec's own test:

**Schedule A line 8**: *"If you didn't use all of your home mortgage loan(s) to buy, build, or improve your home, see instructions and check this box."* btctax fills 8a from `mortgage_interest_1098` (supported per `crates/btctax-cli/LIMITATIONS.md:38`) and never touches the box — the Schedule A filler fills exactly two checkboxes, `check_5a_sales_tax` and `check_18_elects_smaller` (`crates/btctax-forms/src/schedule_a.rs:91-92`). No field, no question, no refusal, no LIMITATIONS entry (grep: zero hits for mixed/proceeds/936). A single box, printed unchecked on every itemizing return with a mortgage — and under §163(h)(3)(F) (2018–2025), interest on proceeds *not* used to buy/build/improve is not deductible at all, so an unchecked box beside a full 8a deduction is an unaffirmed statement **plus an understatement**, for a population squarely inside the "Common W-2 household" archetype. This is *identical in shape* to §2.5's dual-status finding, and r1-I5's standard applies verbatim: a spec claiming class closure must close it (registry question live when Schedule A files with 8a > 0; "No" ⇒ refuse, Pub 936 allocation unmodeled — the exact `DualStatusAlienUnsupported` pattern) or disclaim it in writing with an owning phase.

The root cause is that the spec never states the rule that separates **must-ask single boxes** from **lawful deferrals**. The codebase already practices the distinction: the digital-asset question on a no-activity year prints *both boxes blank* — "btctax never answers 'No'… `false` means unchecked, not answered in the negative" (`return_1040.rs:986-990`) — and Schedule B's FBAR sub-question "stays" blank by documented design (`schedule_b.rs:22`), as do Schedule C's G/I/J pairs. The load-bearing criterion: **a Yes/No pair can be visibly deferred to the filer's pen (both-blank is facially incomplete); a single checkbox cannot (unchecked reads as "No")**. That criterion is why §2.5 is right about dual-status and why the DA question needs no registry entry — and it is nowhere in the spec. Without it, the census is a list, not a closed set, and the next single-box (line 8 today) lands unclassified. **Fix:** state the criterion in §2, do the one-time sweep of the printed checkbox surface (my sweep: 1040 header set — covered; DA pair — deferred-derived; Sch B 7a/8 — covered; FBAR sub-question — deferred, documented; Sch A 5a/18 — filled; **Sch A line 8 — OPEN**; Sch C F — filled; G/I/J pairs — deferrable; H — single box, no tax direction, disclaim as (C); 8960's §6013(g)/(h)/1.1411-10(g) election boxes — opt-in elections, lawful unchecked; 8949/Sch D — data-derived), and close or disclaim line 8.

### I-5 — The build order (§5) is not executable as written: steps 1–3 reference fields that step 4 creates

Step 1 builds `FORM_QUESTIONS` with **7 entries**, whose `get`/`set` for `HsaPresent` need `sch1.hsa_present: Option<bool>` (today a bare `bool` — type error) and whose `DualStatusAlien` entry reads `ri.dual_status_alien` (today **nonexistent** — compile error). Nothing compiles until step 4 runs. Separately, step 2's property test ("blank it ⇒ `screen_inputs` refuses with that entry's reason") cannot go green before step 3 rewires `screen_inputs` — with the corrected `DependentSpouse` liveness, the registry expects a refusal on MFJ-no-spouse-Person that today's hand-written screen (`return_refuse.rs:589`) does not produce; so step 2 ends red, violating the section's own "each step red → green" contract. **Fix:** reorder — field changes first (current step 4, plus the I-1 migration), then registry + anchor, then the screen derivation — and state which step turns the I1 case green.

### I-6 — §2.2's deletion mechanism is factually backwards: there is no `deny_unknown_fields` anywhere, so deletion is a *silent* change, not a breaking one — and it deepens the exact lie it targets

Grep: zero `deny_unknown_fields` in the workspace; `income import` is `toml::from_str` straight into `ReturnInputs` (`crates/btctax-cli/src/cmd/tax.rs:103-105`), and serde ignores unknown keys by default. So after deleting the three fields, a filer's TOML carrying `box13_retirement_plan = true` — a real W-2, box 13 checked, faithfully transcribed — **imports clean and the key vanishes**, with no error, no warning, and (unlike today) no trace even in `income show`. The spec's sentence *"Deleting is a breaking change to the TOML surface; there are no users, so the cost is zero"* is wrong on the mechanism: nothing breaks, which is the problem. §2.2's own indictment of dead fields — *"the user types it, we ignore it"* — describes the post-deletion state *more* accurately than the pre-deletion one. The deletion itself is right (I verified the fields are genuinely unconsumed: `ssn_valid_for_employment` appears only at its two declarations; `box13_retirement_plan` only as `box13_retirement_plan: _` at `return_refuse.rs:237`; and the box-13 direction analysis — §219(g) fail-open, understating — is correct). But the resolution is incomplete without its companion: **spec unknown-key rejection (or a warned-unknown-keys audit) at the TOML import boundary.** Note the design cost honestly: `deny_unknown_fields` on `ReturnInputs` itself would also bind the stored-JSON path and break the module's documented forward-compat discipline (`return_inputs.rs:7-8`), so this needs a CLI-side decision, not an attribute tossed on the core struct.

## The verdicts the task specifically requested

- **`dual_status_alien` (§2.5): SOUND, including live-always.** Verified: the checkbox prints from the MFS coupling alone (`packet.rs:325-326`, filler at `form1040_full.rs:352`), so the dual-status half is silently "No" on every non-MFS return today. §63(c)(6)(B) ("a nonresident alien individual" ⇒ standard deduction zero) is the correct statutory hook — a dual-status person is a nonresident for part of the year, and it is the cite the checkbox itself embodies; `ItemizeElection::Auto` does grant the std deduction. On proportionality: I looked for a cheaper correct answer and there is none — no input signals residency status, a skippable-plus-advisory design leaves the box silently unchecked (the defect itself), and the pen-deferral pattern is unavailable because this is a *single* box (see I-4's criterion — which, once stated, is precisely the justification §2.5 currently lacks). One question per year per filer is the honest price of not silently answering a statement that, wrong, is a false declaration and an understatement together. Keep it.
- **The three DEAD fields: deletion is right** (subject to I-6's mechanism fix). No fidelity argument survives: box 13 captured-but-explicitly-exempt would be exactly the "pre-armed D-8" §2.2 correctly diagnoses, and the classifier forces the field's classified return when §219(g)/§32 land.
- **`BlindBoxForfeitedNotDeclared`: the dollar claim is true.** §63(f)(1)/(f)(2) grant the same additional amount per box; the code holds one `per_box` for all four boxes (`AgedBlindBoxes::count()` × `std_aged_blind_married|unmarried`, $1,550/$1,950 TY2024). The blind box flows from `Person.blind` (`packet.rs:210-215`). The advisory is right to exist; its trigger is I-2.
- **`SalesTaxElectionNotMade`: not well-formed** — no firing predicate at all (I-2), plus the prompt footgun (I-3).
